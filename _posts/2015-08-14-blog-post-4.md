I did not plan to become a researcher. When I started my undergraduate degree in Computer Science and Engineering at Ahsanullah University of Science and Technology, I knew I enjoyed programming — but I had no clear picture of where that would lead. This is the story of how I found my way into Medical Image Processing, published my first paper on arXiv, and what I learned along the way.

## Finding the Problem

The turning point was not a single moment — it was a growing frustration with how AI models are evaluated in medical contexts. I kept reading papers that reported impressive Dice scores on benchmark datasets, yet I could not find a clear answer to a simple question: *what does this model do when it is wrong?* In high-stakes clinical settings, the shape of failure matters as much as average performance.

That question led me to brain metastasis segmentation — a problem where existing models failed in a very specific, very dangerous way. And that became my research.

## Building the Foundation

I had no formal research mentorship at the start. I learned by reading papers, breaking down architectures in code, and running experiments on publicly available datasets. The first few months were largely spent understanding why standard models failed before I had any idea how to fix them.

## The First Paper

Submitting to arXiv for the first time felt significant — not because it meant the work was finished, but because it made it real. Putting your ideas into a structured argument, defending every design choice, and accepting that others will read and critique it is a different kind of learning than coursework.

## What I Would Tell My Earlier Self

Start with a problem that genuinely bothers you, not a method you want to apply. Read critically — a strong Dice score is not the same as a safe model. And do not wait until you feel "ready" to start writing. The writing is part of the thinking.
