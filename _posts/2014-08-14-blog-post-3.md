Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) is the default metric for medical image segmentation — and for good reason. It balances precision and recall into a single, interpretable score. But relying on Dice alone can hide failure modes that matter enormously in clinical practice.

## The Problem with Dice Alone

Dice measures overlap between predicted and ground truth masks. A model can achieve a reasonable Dice score while still producing predictions with wildly inaccurate boundaries. In brain metastasis segmentation, I observed models with moderate Dice scores producing 95% Hausdorff Distances exceeding 150 mm — meaning the worst-case boundary error spanned most of the scan. For radiosurgery planning, this is unacceptable.

## Metrics That Matter

**Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC):** Measures volumetric overlap. Good for assessing overall segmentation quality but insensitive to boundary accuracy.

**95% Hausdorff Distance (HD95):** Measures the worst-case boundary error at the 95th percentile. Critical for surgical planning tasks where boundary precision is non-negotiable.

**Precision:** What fraction of your predicted tumor is actually tumor? Low precision means over-segmentation — the model is predicting healthy tissue as tumor.

**Recall (Sensitivity):** What fraction of actual tumor did the model detect? Low recall means under-segmentation — the model is missing lesions.

## Reporting All Four

In my SG-Net paper, I reported all four metrics independently. This revealed that existing models optimized for Dice were achieving high recall at the cost of precision — exactly the over-segmentation paradox. A model that only reports Dice would have obscured this entirely.

If you are evaluating a medical segmentation model, report DSC, HD95, Precision, and Recall separately. A single number is never the full story.
