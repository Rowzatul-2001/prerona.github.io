Brain tumor segmentation models often look impressive on paper — high sensitivity, strong recall scores, confident predictions. But in clinical practice, a model that segments too much can be just as dangerous as one that misses lesions entirely. This is the **over-segmentation paradox**, and it is something I encountered firsthand while working on brain metastasis segmentation in MRI.

## What Is the Over-Segmentation Paradox?

Standard soft-attention CNNs trained on imbalanced medical datasets tend to overfit toward sensitivity. The model learns that predicting "tumor" aggressively reduces false negatives — which looks good on recall-based metrics — but at the cost of catastrophic precision collapse. In my experiments, precision dropped below 0.23 while boundary errors exceeded 150 mm. For stereotactic radiosurgery, where targeting accuracy must be within millimeters, this is clinically unacceptable.

## Why Does This Happen?

The root cause lies in how soft attention operates. Rather than making hard decisions about which spatial features are relevant, soft attention assigns continuous weights across all regions — including background. When class imbalance is severe (tumor volume < 2% of total), the model compensates by expanding its prediction boundaries, inadvertently capturing healthy tissue.

## How SG-Net Addresses This

I proposed **SG-Net (Spatial Gating Network)**, which replaces soft attention with hard spatial gating mechanisms. Instead of weighting features continuously, SG-Net applies binary-style gating to aggressively suppress background activations. This enforces selectivity at the feature level — the model is structurally constrained from over-predicting, rather than being penalized for it after the fact.

The results were significant: precision improved from 0.20 to **0.52**, and the 95% Hausdorff Distance dropped from 157.52 mm to **56.13 mm** — all while maintaining robust recall (0.79) and using only 0.67M parameters.

## The Broader Lesson

Aggregate metrics like Dice Score can mask dangerous failure modes. A model with a reasonable Dice score can still produce boundaries that are clinically unsafe. Evaluating medical AI requires disaggregated metrics — precision, recall, and boundary accuracy independently — not just a single composite score.

If you are building segmentation models for clinical applications, always ask: *what does failure look like, and is it safe?*
